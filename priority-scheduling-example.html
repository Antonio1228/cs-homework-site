Priority Scheduling Example (Step by Step)

Priority scheduling selects the process with the highest priority first. This example uses non-preemptive priority scheduling where a smaller priority number means higher priority.

Given Processes

Assume all processes arrive at time 0.

Process Burst Time Priority (smaller = higher)
P1 10 3
P2 1 1
P3 2 4
P4 1 5
P5 5 2

Scheduling Order

                    Sort by priority (smallest first):
                    P2 (priority 1) -> P5 (priority 2) -> P1 (priority 3) -> P3 (priority 4) -> P4 (priority 5)

Gantt Chart

                    0   1     6          16   18  19
                    |P2|  P5  |    P1     | P3 |P4|

Completion Time

Process Completion Time
P2 1
P5 6
P1 16
P3 18
P4 19

Turnaround Time

                    Turnaround Time = Completion Time - Arrival Time
                    Arrival Time = 0 for all processes
Process Turnaround Time
P2 1
P5 6
P1 16
P3 18
P4 19

Waiting Time

                    Waiting Time = Turnaround Time - Burst Time
Process Waiting Time
P2 1 - 1 = 0
P5 6 - 5 = 1
P1 16 - 10 = 6
P3 18 - 2 = 16
P4 19 - 1 = 18

Average Waiting Time

                    (0 + 1 + 6 + 16 + 18) / 5 = 41 / 5 = 8.2
                    Average Waiting Time = 8.2

Average Turnaround Time

                    (1 + 6 + 16 + 18 + 19) / 5 = 60 / 5 = 12
                    Average Turnaround Time = 12

Conclusion

Priority scheduling is simple and effective, but it may cause starvation for low-priority processes. Aging can be used to gradually increase the priority of waiting processes to prevent starvation.
